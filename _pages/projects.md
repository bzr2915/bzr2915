Every great revolution in engineering begins with a quiet rearrangement of who serves whom. When the first nomads of the Eurasian steppe broke wild horses to the bridle, they did not merely gain speed; they reconstituted the relationship between human intention and animal power, making the rider not merely a faster traveler but a new category of agent --- the mounted warrior, who overwhelmed infantry and redrew the political map of continents. Thousands of years later, the steam engine performed an inversion still more radical. Muscle --- whether human or equine --- had long been the dominant engine of labor, the force that bent most of the working world to its will. The engine displaced that primacy. A machinist no longer supplied force; he governed a process. **The tool did the work, and the human decided what work was worth doing.** Each of these transitions felt, to contemporaries, like the natural order turning on its head. Each was, in retrospect, an enlargement of what human purpose could accomplish once it was freed from the bottleneck of human capacity.

The history of chip design is precisely such **a story of subject and object trading places**. In the beginning, the designer stood alone at the drafting table, sole agent and sole bottleneck. Then software tools arose and the designer became a wielder of instruments --- still the subject, but amplified. Later, algorithms grew sophisticated enough to propose, optimize, and even surprise their operators, and the tool began to share in the agency it once merely served. Today we stand at the threshold of yet another inversion, perhaps the most consequential of them all: **the moment when artificial intelligence ceases to be an assistant holding the engineer's lamp and becomes, itself, the orchestrating intelligence that conceives, explores, and executes design**. The human does not vanish from this picture, but the locus of initiative shifts.

This page tells that story in four acts, and it tells it because our research is aimed squarely at the latest --- and most profound --- of these inversions. To understand why AI-driven chip design matters, one must first understand what came before: the craftsman's era, the rise of electronic design automation, the long march of machine learning into the design flow, and finally the emergence of autonomous agents that treat the entire toolchain as a medium rather than a master. What follows is not a catalogue of publications but a narrative of ideas --- each act a chapter in the ongoing transformation of who, exactly, is designing the chip.

## The Artisan Era: Human as Sole Agent

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/research/act1_artisan.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" alt="The Artisan Era — a lone engineer at a drafting table designs circuits by hand" %}

In the autumn of 1958, Jack Kilby stood at a workbench in Texas Instruments' Dallas laboratory and assembled the first integrated circuit --- a sliver of germanium with transistors, resistors, and capacitors joined by tiny gold wires. The tools of his trade would have been recognizable to any draftsman of the previous century: pencil, ruler, coordinate paper, a steady hand, and an extraordinary capacity for spatial reasoning. Every wire, every junction, every parasitic interaction lived first in the designer's mind and was then transcribed, laboriously and exactly, onto paper. **The designer's brain was not merely the most important design tool; it was the only design tool.** Creativity and verification alike were cognitive acts. If an error lurked in the layout, no program would flag it; only the engineer's memory of his own intentions could catch the mistake. The ceiling of what could be designed was, in a very literal sense, the ceiling of what one person could think.

For a brief moment this arrangement sufficed. But in 1965 Gordon Moore, then at Fairchild Semiconductor, observed that the number of components on a chip had been doubling roughly every year --- a pace later revised to approximately every two years, but the trajectory was unmistakable. Moore's Law, as it came to be known, described an exponential arc in physical complexity. Human cognition, however, follows no such curve. Working memory is stubbornly finite; attention is sequential; the capacity to hold a thousand interacting constraints in one's head does not double with each calendar year. By the early 1970s, circuits had grown complex enough that no single engineer could any longer carry an entire design from concept to layout in the theater of his own mind. The crisis was not one of physics or fabrication; it was **a crisis of cognition**, a mismatch between the relentless growth of what silicon could contain and the fixed bandwidth of the human intellect. Something had to give --- and the answer, when it came, was not smarter humans but an entirely new kind of tool.

## The Tool Era: Human Operates Machine

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/research/act2_tools.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" alt="The Tool Era — an engineer conducts an orchestra of EDA software tools" %}

That new kind of tool arrived, as revolutions often do, from a university laboratory where theory and necessity happened to share the same corridor. In 1973, at Berkeley, Larry Nagel completed under the guidance of Donald Pederson a program called SPICE --- Simulation Program with Integrated Circuit Emphasis --- and in doing so gave the profession an idea so simple it seemed, in hindsight, inevitable: **simulate before you fabricate**. Before SPICE, the most reliable test of a circuit was to build it and measure what came back. After SPICE, an engineer could interrogate a design mathematically, watching currents and voltages ripple through a model of silicon that existed nowhere but inside a computer's memory. The drafting table did not disappear, but it was no longer the final court of appeal; the simulator became a second pair of eyes, tireless and immune to wishful thinking. A decade later, Aart de Geus and his colleagues at Synopsys introduced the Design Compiler, which could accept a description of logic written in a hardware description language and synthesize from it a gate-level netlist --- translating human intent into structural reality with a speed and consistency no team of hand-mappers could rival. Around the same period, Carl Sechen's TimberWolf system at Berkeley demonstrated that simulated annealing could place and route thousands of cells on a chip with a quality approaching, and sometimes surpassing, what the best human layout artists achieved through intuition alone. One by one, the manual labors of the artisan era were subsumed by algorithms, and the discipline acquired a name to match its new character: **Electronic Design Automation**.

The paradigm that these tools established was clear and, for three decades, remarkably stable. The human remained the subject of every sentence in the design story; the software was the predicate, the obedient verb that carried out the designer's will. An engineer chose the architecture, partitioned the logic, set the timing constraints, selected the optimization strategy, and judged whether the result met specification. The tool executed --- faithfully, rapidly, and at a scale no hand could match --- but **it never proposed, never questioned, never dissented**. SPICE did not suggest a better topology; the place-and-route engine did not argue for a different floorplan. In this division of labor, ninety-nine percent of the physical effort migrated from human hands to software processes, yet the one percent that remained --- the strategic decisions, the creative leaps, the moments of judgment where experience and intuition outweighed any algorithmic rule --- stayed exclusively in the province of the living mind. Tools were, in the end, extraordinarily powerful hammers, but **hammers do not deliberate over what to build**.

For a long while, this compact between human intelligence and machine computation held firm, and it produced wonders: microprocessors of staggering complexity, systems-on-chip integrating billions of transistors, designs that would have struck Jack Kilby as the work of an alien civilization. Yet even as the tools grew more capable, the fundamental grammar of the relationship never changed. Every new generation of EDA software still required a human who understood its inputs, configured its parameters, interpreted its outputs, and bore final responsibility for the design. The engineer's role shifted from draftsman to conductor, but the conductor still stood at the podium, baton in hand, accountable for every note the orchestra played. As transistor counts climbed from millions to billions and design-rule manuals swelled from slim pamphlets to documents of crushing complexity, an uncomfortable question began to press upon the profession with the same quiet insistence that Moore's observation had pressed upon the artisans a generation before: what happens when even the act of configuring, constraining, and judging --- the conductor's own art --- becomes too vast and too intricate for any single human mind to perform?

## The AI-Assisted Era: Putting Eyes on the Hammer

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/research/act3_ai_assisted.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" alt="The AI-Assisted Era — a hammer fitted with AI-powered eyes, still gripped by a human hand" %}

The answer that began to crystallize in the 2010s wore a new name but carried an old ambition: machine learning. Neural networks learned to predict timing closure outcomes without running the full synthesis flow; Bayesian optimization learned to tune circuit parameters with far fewer simulation calls than brute-force search; reinforcement learning agents learned to propose placement strategies by navigating the layout problem as a sequential decision task. To a generation of researchers and venture capitalists alike, this looked like a paradigm shift, a second Copernican turn in which the tool would at last seize the initiative from the toolmaker. But look more carefully at the grammar of these early successes and a subtler picture emerges. The human still defined the design flow, still selected which machine-learning model to deploy at which stage, still interpreted the results and decided whether to accept or override the algorithm's recommendation. **AI was inserted into the existing EDA pipeline as an accelerator** --- a smarter component slotted into an unchanged architecture, much as a superior carburetor might be fitted to an engine whose chassis remained the same. The subject of the sentence remained the human designer and the traditional EDA flow; artificial intelligence was the called object, the function invoked at the designer's discretion. Give the hammer eyes, and it can aim itself with uncanny precision --- but someone still has to pick it up and swing.

The story of our own research begins with a deceptively simple observation about one of the last bastions of manual craftsmanship in chip design: analog circuit synthesis. Unlike digital logic, where synthesis tools had long since automated the path from description to netlist, analog design in the 2010s still depended on the seasoned intuition of human experts who adjusted transistor widths, bias currents, and compensation networks by hand, guided by experience that took years to accumulate and could not easily be written down. At the heart of this craft lay a black-box simulation problem --- propose a set of device parameters, run an expensive SPICE-class simulator whose single evaluation might consume minutes or hours, observe whether the circuit meets its performance specifications, and repeat. The design space was vast, high-dimensional, and punishingly expensive to explore. We asked: how can AI navigate such a space without squandering tens of thousands of simulation hours? Our first answer was Bayesian optimization --- building probabilistic surrogate models of the simulator's input-output behavior and using those surrogates to guide the search intelligently rather than blindly. Early results were encouraging, but dimensionality proved the real adversary: a practical analog circuit may expose fifty to two hundred tunable parameters, and the Gaussian processes that serve as the workhorse of Bayesian optimization scale poorly when dimensions climb into the dozens, let alone the hundreds.

This dimensional wall forced us into a sustained program of methodological invention that would unfold over the better part of a decade. We developed subspace decomposition methods that could identify the low-dimensional structures hidden within the high-dimensional parameter space --- effective manifolds along which the simulator's response varied most sharply --- and confine the surrogate modeling effort to those critical directions, a family of techniques we came to call **tSS-BO** and **BBGP-sDFO**. When even subspace methods reached their limits, we turned to domain decomposition, partitioning the entire search space into regions using Voronoi-tree structures and dispatching local optimizers in parallel across the partition, an approach embodied in **cVTS** and later refined into **D3PBO**. The multi-objective challenge --- optimizing power, speed, area, and noise simultaneously under manufacturing constraints --- demanded new acquisition functions and Pareto-aware search strategies, which led to **VTSMOC** and the hierarchical multi-objective framework we named **HiMOSS**. And because industrial design teams do not have the luxury of running one simulation at a time, we developed batch and asynchronous parallel evaluation schemes capable of keeping dozens of simulator licenses busy simultaneously, converting wall-clock time from an obstacle into an ally. Meanwhile, a parallel thread in the lab explored a fundamentally different paradigm: reinforcement learning. Instead of treating circuit sizing as a one-shot optimization problem to be solved by a surrogate-guided search, we modeled it as a sequential decision process in which an agent learns, through trial and interaction, to adjust parameters iteratively --- much as a seasoned human designer would nudge a bias current upward, observe the effect on bandwidth, and decide the next move. Multi-agent reinforcement learning frameworks, notably our **EVDMARL** system, allowed us to decompose a complex circuit into functional sub-blocks, each managed by a specialized agent, mirroring the way experienced design teams partition their work among specialists who communicate at well-defined interfaces.

The methods we had built for analog circuits proved surprisingly portable, and in the years that followed we carried the same core ideas --- surrogate modeling, intelligent search, decomposition, multi-objective reasoning --- into domains we had not originally anticipated. We applied Bayesian and evolutionary optimization frameworks to microprocessor design-space exploration, navigating the combinatorial wilderness of pipeline depths, cache hierarchies, and branch-predictor configurations in tools we called **APPLE** and **ROI-HIT**. We accelerated physical simulation itself, developing a path-integral random-walk solver for thermal analysis, **pPIRW**, and physics-constrained neural networks for predicting IR-drop across power delivery networks, an approach we named **CRC**. We contributed **AnalogGym**, an open benchmark suite designed to standardize how the field evaluates AI-driven circuit synthesis, because we had learned from hard experience that progress without reproducible comparison is progress in the dark. What had begun as a single question about analog sizing had grown, almost organically, into a systematic toolkit for AI-assisted electronic design automation --- a research program whose coherence lay not in the uniformity of its techniques but in the consistency of its animating question: how do you make expensive black-box design problems tractable through learning?

And yet, for all its breadth, we recognized that our program had been pushing a single paradigm toward something approaching its natural limit. Our algorithms could navigate hundred-dimensional spaces with a parsimony of simulation calls that would have seemed implausible a decade earlier; they could balance competing objectives under nonlinear constraints and transfer knowledge across circuit families so that experience gained on one amplifier shortened the path to the next. The results were real, the industrial collaborations productive, the papers well received. But **the fundamental architecture of the design process had not changed**. AI remained a module --- however sophisticated --- that the human designer plugged into a predetermined flow at a predetermined stage. The human still decided which problem to formulate, which tool to invoke, what "good enough" meant, and when to stop. We had given the hammer extraordinarily refined eyes, eyes that could see around corners and anticipate the grain of the wood. But as the decade drew to a close, a different question began to form in our minds, quieter than the ones we had been asking but far more radical in its implications: **what if the hand on the hammer were no longer human?**

## The AI-Native Era: Subject and Object Reversed

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/research/act4_ai_native.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" alt="The AI-Native Era — an AI brain at a command hub orchestrates design tools while a human supervises from above" %}

That question, once admitted, rearranged everything. To say "AI-Native EDA" is not to say "more AI in EDA" --- not a thicker coat of intelligence painted over the same old scaffolding. It is to say that the grammatical structure of the design sentence has been rewritten. In the world we had known, the human designer was the subject and AI was an instrument the subject occasionally called upon: a faster optimizer here, a smarter predictor there, slotted into a flow whose architecture the human had composed and whose final judgments the human still rendered. In the world now taking shape, **AI is the subject**. It is the intelligence that conceives a design strategy, surveys the available EDA tools as a craftsman surveys the implements on his bench, selects the right instrument for the task at hand, invokes it, interprets the result, and decides --- autonomously --- what to do next. The tools do not disappear; they become the vocabulary of an agent's design language, the verbs through which an artificial mind acts upon silicon. Nor does the human disappear; but the human's role migrates from operator to supervisor, from executor to the one who defines the problem worth solving and the constraints within which a solution must be sought. **Where humans once used AI to assist their tools, now AI uses tools to assist its own design process --- subject and object have traded places.** This is not an incremental improvement in degree; it is a categorical shift in kind, and our recent work has been an attempt to build the first instruments native to the new grammar.

Our first step in this direction was **MARIO**, a meta-optimization framework built on a simple premise: if an AI agent is intelligent enough to run one optimization algorithm well, it ought to be intelligent enough to choose which algorithm to run. MARIO does not commit to a single search strategy the way our earlier Bayesian and reinforcement-learning tools had done. Instead, it maintains a portfolio of optimization algorithms --- evolutionary, surrogate-based, gradient-free, hybrid --- and monitors their performance in real time, dynamically reallocating computational resources toward whichever strategy is making the most progress and withdrawing resources from those that have stalled. In effect, the AI optimizes the optimization process itself.

Building on MARIO, we developed **ATOM**, which pushed the work from parameter tuning into circuit topology. Where our earlier tools asked "given this architecture, what are the best device sizes?", ATOM asks a more fundamental question: "**what architecture should exist in the first place?**" It explores the combinatorial space of how transistors, capacitors, and resistors might be connected, proposing topological candidates that human designers would not readily conceive, and evaluating them with a speed and breadth no single mind could match. MARIO let AI govern its own optimization process; ATOM went further: **deciding the shape of the thing before deciding its dimensions**.

Our most recent work is **Atelier**, an LLM-based multi-agent system in which the entire arc of circuit design --- from specification to verified result --- is carried out by a collaborative society of specialized AI agents. One agent analyzes requirements and decomposes them into sub-problems. Another searches the topology space. A third refines candidate architectures. A fourth tunes parameters. A fifth stands apart as a decision maker, adjudicating trade-offs, accepting or rejecting proposals, and directing the next iteration. These agents communicate, negotiate, and occasionally disagree, much as the members of an experienced human design team would --- but at machine speed, across a breadth of alternatives no human team could simultaneously entertain. **The human provides the specification and the constraints; everything else is AI-directed.**

Looking back across these three systems, what they share is not a single algorithm but a progressive trajectory: AI assumes an increasingly active role in the design loop --- from selecting its own strategy (MARIO), to conceiving architecture (ATOM), to orchestrating the entire flow (Atelier). We do not claim that the human role is thereby diminished; a supervisor who defines the right problem is worth more than a thousand executors who solve the wrong one efficiently. But the locus of initiative --- the place in the loop where the next action is chosen --- has been shifting, quietly and consequentially, from the human intelligence that once held it exclusively to the AI systems our research has spent a decade building. The artisan became a toolmaker; the toolmaker became a conductor; the conductor is becoming a curator who frames the question and judges the answer. **The hand on the hammer is no longer human.**

## The Unfinished Inversion

But has it? To narrate four acts is not to announce a conclusion; it is, at most, to pause at a turn in the road where the vista opens and the path ahead disappears into weather we have not yet learned to read. The inversion described on this page --- tool becoming agent, object becoming subject --- is real in its early demonstrations, yet one must ask with the honesty that scholarship demands whether we are witnessing a completed revolution or merely standing inside the pivot, unable to see how far the turning still has to go. A machine that optimizes a metric with superhuman efficiency is not the same as a mind that judges what constitutes a good design in the fullest sense of that quietly enormous word: not merely a circuit that meets specification, but one whose architecture carries an elegance no loss function was written to reward, one that anticipates the manufacturing risks lurking two process nodes in the future, one that senses, in some fashion we cannot yet formalize, that the market's window is open now and will not stay open long. **Optimization is a verb; judgment is a habit of the soul.** Whether the distance between them can be closed by scale, by architecture, by some technique not yet imagined, remains the defining open question of our field --- and intellectual honesty compels us to hold it open rather than shut it with premature certainty.

Perhaps the truest answer is that the locus of human irreplaceability has not vanished but migrated once more, as it did at every prior inversion, to a higher stratum of abstraction: not executing, not even orchestrating, but asking. **Asking the question is more fundamental than answering it.** No algorithm we know of has learned to perform the one act that precedes all design --- the act of deciding which questions are worth posing, which problems deserve the finite attention of a civilization. And here Qian Mu's quiet admonition returns with undiminished force: ***without knowing whence we came, we cannot know where we go***. The four acts told on this page are not the end of this story; they are the context without which the next act would be unintelligible. What shape that act will take, we do not know. We know only that the question of who serves whom will be asked again, in a form none of us can yet imagine, and that the asking itself --- patient, honest, unfinished --- is the surest sign that the work remains alive.
